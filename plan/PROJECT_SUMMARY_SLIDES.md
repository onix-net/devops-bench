# devops-bench

### The trusted scorecard for AI agents that run cloud infrastructure.

## Slide 1: measure DevOps agents on the work they actually do, in a repeatable way

**DevOps is huge. The way to lead is to measure agents on the real work, the same way every time.**

- The space is enormous: deployments, incidents, scaling, networking, security, and cost, across Kubernetes and the broader cloud. There are many ways to judge how agents perform.
- Our goal: put agents into the scenarios they actually face in day-to-day operations, and measure how well they handle them.
- Trustworthy and comparable: scenarios are built in predictable, repeatable ways, and performance is scored in predictable, repeatable ways. Same performance, same score, every time.
- Hard to game: scenarios vary each run, and the agent never sees the answer key.
- Why Google should care: owning a neutral, trusted standard shapes the market, and broad, fair coverage makes Google Cloud and Kubernetes strengths obvious without looking biased.

## Slide 2: the plan and the payoff

**A small team plus an AI "factory" ships a credible first version in 6 to 8 weeks.**

- How we build it: one lead and two engineers set direction and quality. Agents mass-produce and auto-check the scenarios.
- Scope: about 120 real-world scenarios. Deep on Google Cloud and Kubernetes first, with AWS and Azure to follow on the same foundation.
- Timeline: 6 to 8 weeks to the firest ~120 scenarios.
- The payoff: a defensible industry standard Google can anchor to.
- Cheap to scale: authoring a new scenario or group of scenarios is relatively fast. An agent is able to execute the bulk of the work with humans defining the goals and checking the scenarios are evaluating the right thing, have a credible difficulty rating, and the solution makes sense- not weeks of engineering.
- Neutral credibility: the test is fair and hard to game, so its verdict is trusted.

## Speaker notes

DevOps and DevOps agents span a huge range of real work, from deployments and incidents to scaling, networking, security, and cost, across Kubernetes and the broader cloud. The goal of this project is to put agents into the scenarios they actually encounter day to day and measure how well they handle them. What makes the results trustworthy and comparable is that scenarios are both built and scored in predictable, repeatable ways, so the same performance always earns the same score. It is also hard to game, because scenarios vary each run and the agent never sees the answer key. For Google, owning a neutral, trusted standard shapes the market, and broad, fair coverage makes the Google Cloud and Kubernetes strengths obvious without looking biased.
